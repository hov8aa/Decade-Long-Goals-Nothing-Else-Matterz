That gap is not a bug in the project. It might be the dataset.

I am the scientist, the subject, and the data here — so every pause gets logged with the same precision as every commit. If the hypothesis is that small goals compound into decade-long ones, then the record of how often I return to this repo is the first experiment.

## v0.1 — 10 Minutes Goals (2026-06-11)

The first version asked one question: can I do one thing for 10 minutes without anything else mattering?

Type a goal. Start a full-screen countdown. Finish it or give up — both get recorded. Every day starts fresh; nothing carries over except the option to pick an old goal back up.

Built in one session. Then stopped.

## The pause (2026-06-11 → 2026-07-05)

Why did it stop? My honest account: Fable was disabled — the US government took it off the board — and without it I felt no urge to build. Twenty-four days, zero commits.

But wait. The uncomfortable observation isn't that a tool went away. It's that my urge to build my own consistency system was coupled to the availability of someone else's model. The app was supposed to make *me* the engine. The pause revealed the engine was partly outsourced.

I'm not resolving that here. I'm logging it. Fable 5 arrived, the urge came back the same day, and I'm choosing to treat that correlation as data rather than embarrassment.

## v0.2 — 10 Decade-Long Goals (2026-07-05)

The rename is the hypothesis.

v0.1 treated 10-minute goals as the core. Wrong altitude. The core is the decade-long goals — the handful of things that define the best version of me by 2036. Ten of them, at most. The 10-minute goal is the bare minimum unit of action that keeps a decade-long goal alive today.

So the structure inverts:

- **Decade goals are the spine.** You name them first. There can only ever be ten.
- **10-minute goals are the heartbeat.** Every session belongs to a decade goal. You don't do 10 minutes of *something* — you do 10 minutes of *one of the ten*.
- **The protagonist** (me first, then whoever else picks this up) acts on the smallest unit to serve the largest one.

A 10-minute session that serves nothing decade-long is just a timer. A decade goal with no 10-minute sessions is just a wish. The app now lives in the space between those two failure modes.

Whether 10 minutes a day can actually carry a decade — that's the experiment. Check the frequency table above to see how it's going.

## v0.3 — The North Star (2026-07-06)

v0.2 lived one day. Building it revealed the misreading: "10 Decade-Long Goals" was never ten goals. It's one goal, and the ten was always the minutes.

So v0.3 inverts again, harder. **One decade-long goal.** Not ten, not a list — one, and the code physically cannot hold two. The timeline underneath (a life as successive decade chapters) exists in storage, invisible, waiting years to matter.

What earned its place this version:

- **The commitment ceremony.** Setting the goal stopped being a form. Terms stated like a contract — 3,653 days charged whether you commit or not, ×100 paid only on persistence, no refunds. Then you retype the goal by hand. Then you hold a button for ten unbroken seconds. Release early, it resets. The entry fee is paid in the only coin this app deals in.
- **Evolution with reasoning, never replacement.** The goal can be rewritten, but the old wording and its why stay in the trail forever. Reading the trail costs one tap. Changing it costs ten, inside two-second windows. The asymmetry is the design.
- **The rule of tens** surfaced on its own: 10 seconds to commit, 10 taps to evolve, 10 minutes to act, 10 years to arrive.
- **Spec before code.** 19 requirements, 8 named exceptions, 41 tests written red before a line of implementation. The suite went green without editing a test.

Note the frequency table: two consecutive days for the first time in this repo's life. The app about showing up is finally being built like someone shows up for it. Whether that holds — same experiment, same table.
